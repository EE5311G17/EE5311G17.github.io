How do we recover a 3D world from a stack of 2D photos? And why did NeRF and 3D Gaussian Splatting suddenly make that question feel much less impossible?

Take a few dozen photos while walking around a room. Every image is flat: just a grid of pixels. Yet your brain has no trouble imagining where the chair sits, how far the wall is, or what the scene would look like from a slightly different viewpoint. Modern scene reconstruction methods try to do the same thing computationally: infer a hidden 3D scene from many 2D observations.

This is exactly the kind of problem that makes differentiable computing exciting. We know how images are formed forward: a scene, viewed by a camera, produces a photo. What we want is to run that process in reverse: given the photos, recover the scene. That is an inverse problem. And once the rendering process is differentiable, we can optimize the unknown scene representation directly by gradient descent.

In this blog, we will build that story from the ground up. We will first introduce the camera model, because every reconstruction method quietly stands on it. Then we will use a single inverse-problem lens to understand two influential methods: NeRF and 3D Gaussian Splatting. Both solve scene reconstruction by differentiable optimization, but they make very different design choices about how a scene should be represented and rendered. NeRF treats a scene as a continuous neural field. 3DGS represents it as a cloud of optimized 3D Gaussians. One feels elegant and implicit; the other feels explicit and fast.

By the end, the main takeaway is simple: both methods solve the same problem, but they choose different answers to a deeper question – What is the best differentiable representation of a 3D scene?

1. Reconstructing 3D Scenes from Images

At first glance, scene reconstruction sounds like magic.

We only observe images, not depth, geometry, density, or surface shape directly. A photo does not come with labels like “this point is 2.3 meters away” or “that highlight belongs to a shiny table.” All of that structure is hidden behind the imaging process.

That is why the task is naturally phrased as an inverse problem.

The forward process is straightforward:

The inverse process is what we actually want:

This reversal is difficult because information is lost when 3D is projected to 2D. Different 3D arrangements can sometimes produce similar images. Occlusions hide parts of the scene. Repeated textures confuse correspondence. Lighting and view-dependent effects complicate matters further. So we should not think of scene reconstruction as “decoding the correct answer” from an image. It is better understood as searching for a scene that explains the observations well.

That phrasing already sounds like optimization, which is exactly where EE5311 enters the picture. If we can define a forward renderer that maps a candidate scene to an image, and if that renderer is differentiable, then we can compare rendered images with observed images, compute a loss, and optimize the scene parameters with automatic differentiation.

This is the unifying idea behind both NeRF and 3D Gaussian Splatting.

3. A Common Differentiable Reconstruction Pipeline

With that forward model in place, both NeRF and 3DGS can be understood through the same four-step loop:

  1. Choose a scene representation.
  2. Render an image from a camera pose.
  3. Compare it with the ground-truth image.
  4. Backpropagate the loss and update the scene parameters.

This is why these methods belong so naturally in a differentiable computing course. The entire system is one computational graph. The camera parameters define rays or projections. The renderer maps the current scene estimate to pixels. The image loss quantifies error. Automatic differentiation pushes gradients back to the scene parameters.

The two methods differ mainly in three places:

  • How the scene is represented
  • How the image is rendered
  • Which parameters are optimized

Everything else follows from those choices.

NeRF chooses an implicit neural representation: a multilayer perceptron answers queries about any location in space. 3DGS chooses an explicit representation: many anisotropic Gaussians occupy 3D space directly and are projected to the screen. NeRF renders by integrating along rays. 3DGS renders by splatting projected Gaussians with alpha compositing.

Same inverse problem. Different differentiable worlds.

4.3 Why NeRF felt revolutionary

NeRF made a continuous scene representation actually work for photorealistic novel view synthesis. It did not need a mesh, did not require hand-crafted geometry extraction, and produced views with remarkable consistency.

Its strengths are easy to appreciate:

NeRF is elegant. It is continuous. It can represent fine geometric and appearance detail. And because the color can depend on viewing direction, it can reproduce view-dependent effects such as highlights better than many older models.

But the same design that makes NeRF elegant also makes it expensive.

Every pixel requires many samples. Every sample requires a neural network query. Every rendered image requires a huge amount of ray marching and compositing. In short, NeRF is not just rendering an image—it is repeatedly asking a neural network what the world looks like, point by point, along every ray.

That is why the original NeRF is famous both for its quality and for its slowness.

5. 3D Gaussian Splatting: Fast Reconstruction with Explicit Primitives

If NeRF asks, “What if a scene is a continuous function?”, then 3D Gaussian Splatting asks a more pragmatic question:

What if we represent the scene directly as a set of optimized 3D primitives that are easy to render fast?

This is the spirit of 3DGS.

Instead of an MLP, the scene is represented by a large collection of 3D Gaussians. Each Gaussian carries parameters such as:

  • a center position,
  • a scale and orientation, often encoded by a covariance,
  • an opacity,
  • and appearance coefficients, often using spherical harmonics for view-dependent color.

You can think of each Gaussian as a tiny soft ellipsoid floating in space. One Gaussian is not very expressive. But thousands—or millions—of them can approximate a full scene surprisingly well.

5.1 Why Gaussians are a clever choice

A Gaussian is soft, differentiable, and projection-friendly. That matters a lot.

When a 3D Gaussian is projected onto the image plane, it becomes a 2D elliptical footprint. That means the method can reason directly about which pixels a Gaussian influences. Instead of marching rays through empty space and querying a network over and over, 3DGS projects the primitives that already exist and blends them efficiently.

This makes the whole pipeline feel much closer to rasterization than to volumetric ray integration.

5.2 Rendering in 3DGS

The rendering idea is conceptually simple:

A 3D Gaussian is projected into screen space. Its projected footprint covers a region of the image. The renderer computes how much that Gaussian contributes to nearby pixels. Multiple Gaussians that overlap a pixel are then composited by depth-aware alpha blending.

So instead of saying,

“For this pixel, let me walk through space and ask what is there,”

3DGS says,

“Here are the things already living in space—let me project them and paint their contributions onto the image.”

That shift is the key to its speed.

5.3 The role of anisotropy

The original 3DGS paper uses anisotropic Gaussians rather than simple isotropic blobs. That means each primitive can stretch differently in different directions. This is important because a scene is not made of little spheres. Thin edges, slanted surfaces, and elongated structures all benefit from directional flexibility.

A single anisotropic Gaussian can approximate a local patch much better than a round one.

5.5 Why 3DGS took off so quickly

3DGS became popular because it offered a striking combination: high-quality reconstruction with much faster rendering, often approaching real time.

Its strengths are practical and immediate. It is faster to train than classical NeRF. It is much faster to render. It is explicit enough to feel controllable, yet flexible enough to capture complex appearance.

Its limitations are also worth noting. The representation can be memory-heavy. Training includes engineering details such as densification and pruning that matter a lot in practice. And compared with the clean implicit elegance of NeRF, 3DGS can feel more heuristic.

Still, as a differentiable scene representation, it is one of the most impactful ideas to follow NeRF.

6. NeRF vs 3DGS: What Really Differs?

It is tempting to summarize the story as “NeRF is slower, 3DGS is faster.” That is true, but it is not the full story. The deeper difference is philosophical.

NeRF and 3DGS make different choices about what should be optimized in order to explain images. NeRF says: optimize a continuous neural field. 3DGS says: optimize a set of explicit 3D primitives. That single choice changes almost everything downstream.

NeRF is implicit. The scene is hidden in neural network weights. If you want to know what exists at a point in space, you query the network. This gives NeRF continuity and elegance. Space is represented as a function, not a list of objects.

3DGS is explicit. The scene is directly stored as many Gaussian primitives with locations, sizes, opacities, and colors. If you want to know what exists in space, the representation already contains physical-looking elements you can point to.

NeRF renders by sampling along rays and aggregating through volume rendering. This is mathematically natural for continuous fields, but computationally expensive.

3DGS renders by projecting and splatting primitives. This is closer to fast graphics pipelines and avoids much of the wasted computation in empty space.

NeRF optimizes mainly network weights. 3DGS optimizes geometric and appearance parameters of explicit primitives, often while changing the number of primitives during training.

These differences lead to the trade-offs practitioners actually care about. NeRF often feels more principled as a continuous representation. It has a clean functional form and is historically the method that established neural radiance fields as a major direction. 3DGS often feels more practical. It is designed for speed, easier interactive rendering, and direct GPU-friendly implementation.

In one line:

NeRF asks the network to describe the world everywhere. 3DGS places many soft, optimized pieces directly into the world and renders them efficiently.

If we compress the contrast even further:

  • NeRF: implicit, neural, continuous, slower but conceptually elegant.
  • 3DGS: explicit, primitive-based, fast, highly practical.

For an EE5311-style takeaway, the important point is this: both rely on differentiable rendering and automatic differentiation, but they make different choices about how to parameterize a scene so that gradients become useful.

That is the real comparison.

7.3 Scale Alignment and Pose Normalization in Real-World Scenes

When processing custom video data, the model initially rendered completely black images, and the validation PSNR stagnated at a very low level. This phenomenon stems from the mismatch between the real-world coordinate system and the network’s predefined spatial scale.

The camera poses extracted by COLMAP lack an absolute physical scale; their origin positions and camera distances are arbitrary. However, we set fixed integration bounds for volumetric rendering in the code (e.g., near=2.0, far=6.0). Without spatial intervention, the sampling rays emitted by the network might completely miss the target object. This causes the vast majority of sampling points to fall into empty space without solid entities, thereby failing to compute valid density and color gradients.

To resolve this issue, a Pose Normalization preprocessing step must be introduced. We first determined the scene’s centroid by calculating the average of all camera coordinates and translated it to the origin (0,0,0) of the coordinate system. Subsequently, the entire scene was proportionally scaled based on the maximum distance from the real cameras to the origin, ensuring that all cameras were distributed within the predefined effective integration radius. This step aligns the unbounded physical data with the network’s finite integration bounds.

7.4 Rendering Compromise: First-Person Fly-Through

During the final video rendering and presentation phase, we found that directly applying a standard mathematical spherical trajectory to generate a 360-degree orbit video resulted in noticeable geometry clipping and intersection issues.

The reason for this phenomenon is that the generation of a standard spherical trajectory assumes the Y-axis of the coordinate system is absolutely vertical and the physical center of the object is exactly at the origin. However, in actual SfM reconstruction results, the estimated gravity direction is often tilted, and the geometric centroid of the camera trajectory usually deviates from the object’s actual physical center. Under this unaligned coordinate system, forcing an ideal mathematical circular trajectory causes the virtual camera to deviate from the effective observation area, or even cut directly into the interior of the object, interrupting the ray integration.

Considering the stability of the algorithm and aiming to avoid overly complex coordinate system Principal Component Analysis (PCA) calibration, we settled for a first-person Fly-through rendering approach as a practical alternative. This approach directly utilizes and smooths the real camera trajectory extracted by COLMAP for rendering. This method bypasses the coordinate system alignment problem and ensures that the virtual camera always remains within the effective space previously observed by the model. Consequently, it effectively avoids background artifacts (Floaters) and clipping sensations, presenting the 3D scene features learned by the model more objectively.

7.5 Computational Limitations of Vanilla NeRF and Cutting-Edge Optimizations of Instant-NGP

Although this experiment successfully achieved the implicit reconstruction of 3D scenes, the reproduction process fully exposed the severe computational limitations of the Vanilla NeRF architecture. In implicit neural representations based purely on Multilayer Perceptrons (MLPs), rendering a single low-resolution image requires hundreds of thousands of forward inference calculations along the rays. This approach of forcibly compressing the color and density information of the entire 3D space into the weights of a single neural network causes the training time to grow exponentially when facing high-resolution or large-scale scenes, resulting in extremely low inference efficiency.

Addressing this computational bottleneck, cutting-edge technologies represented by NVIDIA’s Instant-NGP provide a highly valuable engineering optimization approach: trading space for time. Instant-NGP abandons the traditional path of relying solely on massive MLPs to fit high-frequency features and innovatively introduces the Multi-resolution Hash Grid data structure.

This technology divides the 3D space into multi-level discrete grids and uses a hardware-friendly hash table to rapidly look up local spatial features. Subsequently, only an extremely lightweight MLP (typically two layers) is required for decoding and rendering. This Hybrid Representation method, which combines explicit data structures with implicit neural rendering, significantly reduces computational complexity. It cleverly aligns with the low-level memory access optimizations of modern GPUs, successfully compressing the NeRF training cycle from hours or even days to seconds, fundamentally solving the pain point of applying implicit radiance fields in practical engineering.

8.3 Unlocking Geometric Details with Adaptive Density Control

The most defining characteristic of 3DGS is its ability to dynamically alter its explicit topology—spawning new Gaussians where detail is lacking and culling those that are redundant. We rigorously tracked this evolutionary mechanism during training.

Cloning vs. Splitting

Observing the Growth of Gaussian Splats chart, we can empirically track this topological evolution. The curve demonstrates a rapid, stair-step escalation in the total population of Gaussians, particularly during the early-to-mid training iterations. This aggressive growth directly visualizes the densification algorithm actively resolving spatial inaccuracies through two primary operations: cloning and splitting.

When a Gaussian exhibits a large positional gradient—indicating under-reconstruction in a complex, high-frequency region—the system clones it and shifts the duplicate along the gradient vector to fill the geometric void. Conversely, if a Gaussian becomes disproportionately large in an attempt to cover highly varied geometry—indicating over-reconstruction—the system forcefully splits it into two smaller, highly localized Gaussians.

The Necessity of Pruning
Unchecked growth leads to geometric noise and memory bloat. To counteract the aggressive densification, the algorithm routinely executes a pruning phase by resetting the opacity of all Gaussians to a near-zero threshold at scheduled intervals. Splats that fail to regain sufficient opacity during subsequent optimization steps, or those that grow excessively large, are aggressively culled from the population. This continuous, self-regulating cycle of targeted expansion (cloning/splitting) and rigorous refinement (pruning) ensures that the final 3D model maintains a highly optimized, lean topological structure, where every surviving Gaussian contributes meaningfully to the final rendering fidelity.

8.4 Winning the VRAM Battle and Engineering Around Hardware Limits

During local prototyping, we hit a massive hardware roadblock. Our local machine was equipped with an RTX 3050 Laptop GPU, which has a strict 4GB VRAM ceiling. Around iteration 2000 on the standard Tanks and Temples dataset, the explosive proliferation of Gaussians triggered severe Out-of-Memory (OOM) states and system thrashing, completely stalling the training loop.

Rather than immediately throwing expensive cloud compute at the problem, we implemented rigorous software-level constraints to force convergence within the 4GB limit:

  • Programmatic Downsampling: We dynamically resized the training images to a scale=4 factor, ensuring the dimensions (496x272) remained perfectly divisible by 16 to satisfy the parallel tiling requirements of the rasterizer.
  • SH Degree Truncation: We aggressively pruned the memory footprint of the Gaussians by dropping the maximum Spherical Harmonics degree (max_sh_degree) from 3 to 1. This strategic concession slashed the color feature payload from 48 floating-point values down to just 12 per Gaussian.

These dual interventions successfully bypassed the VRAM bottleneck. We observed the scene evolve from an Amorphous Phase (blurry, oversized splats minimizing macroscopic L1 loss), to a Scaffolding Phase (silhouettes emerging), and finally to a Refinement Phase where mechanical micro-geometry was clearly delineated, all within 4GB of VRAM.

9. Applications

Up to this point, we have framed NeRF and 3DGS as solutions to the inverse problem of differentiable scene reconstruction, unpacked their core design choices, and explored how variants extend their performance and capabilities. But the impact of these methods extends far beyond academic research: their end-to-end differentiable pipeline and high-fidelity scene modeling have unlocked tangible, real-world engineering value across a wide range of industries. For computer engineering practitioners, these methods solve long-standing pain points in 3D scene capture and rendering, replacing costly, labor-intensive traditional workflows with flexible, image-based reconstruction. Below, we break down their core applications across key domains, and highlight how the inherent trade-offs of NeRF and 3DGS make each suited to distinct engineering use cases.

9.1 Computer Vision and Computer Graphics

For computer vision and graphics, the core value of differentiable scene reconstruction lies in lowering the barrier to high-quality 3D content creation, a task that once required professional 3D artists, specialized scanning hardware, and months of manual work.

Novel view synthesis: These methods enable free-viewpoint video and virtual camera movement for film, sports broadcast, and archival content, filling in unseen perspectives from a small set of captured images without reshooting.

Digital twins: They can reconstruct photorealistic, geometrically accurate digital replicas of real-world environments, from small indoor spaces to full urban landscapes, for simulation, monitoring, and urban planning.

3D content generation for games and animation: They automate the creation of 3D scene assets from reference photos, eliminating the need for manual modeling of complex environments and props.

In this domain, NeRF is preferred for static scenes with strict requirements for fine detail and photorealism, such as film-grade scene reproduction and high-precision asset creation, where offline rendering time is acceptable for pixel-perfect results. 3DGS excels in use cases requiring real-time rendering and engine integration, such as interactive game environments and live-updating digital twin visualizations, where its GPU-friendly splatting pipeline delivers smooth, low-latency performance.

9.2 Robotics

For robotics, reliable scene understanding is the foundation of safe and autonomous operation, and differentiable reconstruction addresses critical limitations of traditional perception pipelines. Traditional SLAM (Simultaneous Localization and Mapping) systems typically produce only sparse point clouds or low-fidelity meshes, failing to capture fine geometric details, photometric properties, or occluded regions of an environment. NeRF and 3DGS enable dense, high-fidelity 3D scene reconstruction directly from a robot’s on-board camera stream, providing a complete model of the environment for:

  • Scene perception and obstacle detection, with accurate modeling of object geometry and surface properties even in texture-less regions.
  • SLAM integration, with end-to-end differentiable optimization of both camera poses and scene structure, improving localization accuracy in complex environments.
  • Autonomous navigation, with a rich, updatable scene model that supports path planning and dynamic obstacle avoidance.

For robotics use cases, NeRF is suited to offline, high-precision scene mapping, such as pre-scanning factory floors or laboratory environments for robotic manipulation tasks, where maximum geometric and textural accuracy is required. 3DGS is ideal for real-time, on-board perception and navigation, as its fast reconstruction and rendering pipeline supports live scene updates as the robot moves, with minimal computational overhead for edge hardware.

Conclusion

What makes NeRF and 3D Gaussian Splatting so transformative is not just that they answer the question we opened with—how to recover a 3D world from a stack of 2D photos. It is that they redefined the problem itself, framing 3D scene reconstruction as a fully differentiable inverse problem that can be solved end-to-end with gradient descent.

The core recipe we have unpacked throughout this blog is powerful and elegantly simple. It starts with the foundational pinhole camera model, which defines how 3D points become pixels. It requires a choice of scene representation, a differentiable renderer to map that representation to an image, a loss function to compare rendered results to real observations, and automatic differentiation to backpropagate error and refine the scene. This four-step loop is the unifying backbone of both NeRF and 3DGS, and it is the same framework that breaks the limitations of traditional, non-differentiable reconstruction pipelines.

NeRF turned this recipe into a revolution with its continuous, implicit neural radiance field. It abandoned the discrete meshes, voxels, and point clouds of traditional methods, instead encoding the entire scene into a neural network that answers queries about any point in 3D space. Our hands-on implementation of TinyNeRF validated the power of this paradigm: even a lightweight 8-layer MLP, paired with positional encoding and differentiable volume rendering, can reconstruct a photorealistic 3D scene from real-world smartphone footage. This implementation also laid bare the core trade-off of NeRF’s design: its elegant continuous representation delivers exceptional detail, but at the cost of heavy computational overhead, a challenge that has driven a wave of optimized variants from Instant NGP to Mip-NeRF.

3D Gaussian Splatting retained the same differentiable optimization spirit, but reimagined the scene representation for practicality and speed. By replacing the implicit neural field with explicit, anisotropic 3D Gaussians, it swapped the expensive ray marching and per-sample network queries of NeRF for fast, GPU-friendly projection and splatting. This single design choice unlocked real-time rendering while retaining near-NeRF quality, making differentiable scene reconstruction feasible for interactive applications from AR/VR to robotic navigation. Like NeRF, it has spawned a rich ecosystem of extensions, from dynamic scene models to lightweight variants for edge devices.

The real takeaway, as we have emphasized, is not “which method is better.” The better question is: what kind of scene representation makes the inverse problem of 3D reconstruction easiest to optimize for a given use case? NeRF’s implicit, continuous representation is the gold standard for static, high-detail tasks like cultural heritage digitization and film-grade asset creation, where offline rendering time is an acceptable trade-off for pixel-perfect fidelity. 3DGS’s explicit, primitive-based design is the clear choice for real-time, interactive applications, where low latency and fast rendering are non-negotiable. They are not competing solutions, but complementary paradigms that address different ends of the engineering spectrum.

Together, NeRF and 3DGS lay bare the transformative power of differentiable programming. For decades, 3D reconstruction was a fragmented, multi-stage process limited by hand-crafted features and discrete scene representations. But once we model the entire image formation process as a differentiable function, the hard problem of recovering hidden 3D structure from 2D observations becomes an optimization problem we can systematically solve.

From our TinyNeRF implementation on real-world data to the cutting-edge variants pushing the boundaries of speed and scale, every part of this story reinforces the same lesson: the most impactful advances in computer vision and graphics come not from incremental tweaks to existing pipelines, but from reimagining core problems through the lens of differentiable computing. NeRF and 3DGS have already redefined what is possible with image-based 3D reconstruction, and their complementary strengths will continue to shape the future of digital content creation, robotics, AR/VR, and beyond.
